# Federalism as Scale Discipline

## Subtitle

One Constitutional Body, Real Nested Systems, and the Person Carried Across Scale

## What is this?

This is a civic foundation artifact about Federalism. It argues that Federalism is best understood as constitutional scale discipline: the discipline by which one constitutional body remains one without becoming flat, real nested systems remain real without becoming separate worlds, and the person remains carried across scale as a full member of the whole.

## Why should I care?

Federalism is often framed as a contest over which government wins: federal power, state power, local authority, preemption, anti-commandeering, incorporation, civil-rights enforcement, and state sovereignty. This artifact asks a deeper question: what happens to the human being as public power changes scale?

## What is the claim?

The central claim is that Federalism is not primarily the fight over which government wins. Federalism is the scale discipline hosted by the constitutional civic field. It asks whether public power, as it changes scale, still carries the person through real nested systems as part of one constitutional body.

## What is this not?

This is not legal advice, a replacement for Federalism doctrine, constitutional law, state-sovereignty doctrine, preemption doctrine, anti-commandeering doctrine, incorporation doctrine, civil-rights enforcement doctrine, separation-of-powers analysis, or case-specific legal analysis. It also does not claim to resolve tribal sovereignty, private delegated public power, immigration, emergency authority, military force, territories, commerce, or market-union questions by Federalism alone.

## How was this done?

The artifact uses the Structural Flow / Constitutional Physiology / Constitutional Civic Field frame to identify Federalism as a scale discipline. It tests that object through familiar doctrine surfaces, including incorporation, anti-commandeering, preemption, state sovereignty, local control, national power, civil-rights enforcement, and separation of powers. It also develops a failure profile for Federalism: dissolution, shear, drift, echo, and lock.
